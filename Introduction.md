Welcome to the world of tabletop role-playing games! In these games, you and your friends will take on the roles of characters in a shared story, guided by a game master who sets the scene and mediates the action.

Tabletop role-playing games, also known as pen-and-paper games or tabletop RPGs, have been around for decades and have a rich history and tradition. They can be played with just a few people, or with larger groups of up to dozens of players, though I recommend a group of three to five players.

In a tabletop RPG, you will create a character with their own unique abilities and traits, and work with your fellow players to explore a fictional world, overcome challenges, and tell an epic story together. Whether you are a seasoned veteran or new to the world of tabletop RPGs, there is a place for you at the table.

So grab some dice, a pen and some paper, and let's get started!

# How to Play
## Players and the DM
In a tabletop role-playing game, the game master (GM) and the players each have distinct roles and responsibilities.

The game master, also known as the DM (dungeon master), is responsible for creating and running the game. This includes setting the scene, describing the environment and NPCs (non-player characters), and mediating the actions of the players. The DM is also responsible for creating and maintaining the game's story and plot, and for guiding the game's progression.

The players, on the other hand, are responsible for creating and playing their own characters. This includes choosing their character's abilities, traits, and appearance, and role-playing their character's actions and interactions within the game world. Players are also responsible for working together as a team and collaborating with the DM to create a fun and engaging game experience.

Overall, the DM and the players work together to create and play through an immersive and engaging role-playing game.

## The Core Gameplay
The core game loop of a tabletop role-playing game (also known as a pen-and-paper game) typically consists of the following steps:

1.  Setting the scene: The game master (GM) sets the scene by describing the environment and any relevant NPCs (non-player characters) that the players will encounter.
    
2.  Player action: The players describe the actions that their characters will take in response to the scene and the NPCs. This might involve talking to NPCs, exploring the environment, or taking other actions.
    
3.  GM response: The GM describes the results of the players' actions and any consequences that may arise. This might involve NPC reactions, challenges or obstacles that the players must overcome, or other events that unfold.
    
4.  Resolution: The GM and the players determine the outcome of any challenges or conflicts that arise. This usually involves rolling a 20-sided die (a d20) to determine success or failure.
    
5.  Repeat: The process is repeated until the end of the game session or until the players achieve their goals.
    

This basic structure of setting the scene, player action, GM response, and resolution is a common element of many tabletop role-playing games.

> Matt the DM, Liam, Laura and Marisha play in a session where they adventure into a dense forest.

> "Great! Liam, give me a stealth check! Laura, roll animal handling."
> Liam rolls a 5 even with his +5 in stealth this isn't a great result. "Sorry mate, your legs are still clearly looking out from behind that tree." Matt describes the resolution. "Laura what did you get?"
> "I rolled a 20 +3" Laura says with a smirk. "Now that's a roll... The wolf actually seems to calm down for now. It's still a wild animal that might strike at any moment but currently it seems more curious than hungry. How do you want to continue? Marisha? Any ideas?"

## The Social Contract
The social contract is a set of shared expectations and agreements that govern the behavior of players and the game master in a tabletop role-playing game. It is an important aspect of the game and helps to ensure that everyone has a fun and enjoyable experience.

The social contract between the players and the game master, also known as the DM, usually includes the following elements:

1.  Respect: Players and the DM should respect each other's boundaries and preferences, and refrain from any behavior that might make others feel uncomfortable or disrespected.
    
2.  Collaboration: Players and the DM should work together to create a fun and engaging game experience for everyone. This includes being open to suggestions and ideas, and being willing to compromise when necessary.
    
3.  Inclusivity: Players and the DM should strive to create a welcoming and inclusive environment for everyone, regardless of their background or identity.
    
4.  Safety: Players and the DM should prioritize the safety and well-being of everyone involved in the game. This includes respecting each other's physical and emotional boundaries, and being mindful of triggers and sensitive topics.
    

By adhering to these principles, players and the DM can create a positive and enjoyable gaming experience for everyone.

## Session Zero
Session Zero, also known as a character creation session or pre-game session, is a meeting that takes place before the start of a tabletop role-playing game. It is a time for players to create their characters, discuss the game's theme and setting, and establish the social contract between the players and the game master.

While it might seem like Session Zero is not necessary, it is actually an important part of the game preparation process and can help to ensure that everyone is on the same page and has a clear understanding of what to expect.

Some reasons why a Session Zero is recommended include:

1.  Character creation: Session Zero is a good opportunity for players to work with the game master and their fellow players to create their characters. This can help to ensure that everyone has a clear understanding of their character's abilities and motivations, and that the characters fit well within the game's theme and setting.
    
2.  Setting and theme: Session Zero is a good time for the game master to discuss the setting and theme of the game with the players. This can help to ensure that everyone has a clear understanding of the world in which the game takes place and the tone and style of the game.
    
3.  Social contract: Session Zero is a good time for the game master and players to establish the social contract, which is a set of shared expectations and agreements that govern the behavior of everyone in the game. This helps to ensure that everyone has a fun and enjoyable experience.
    

When everyone is on the same page already a session zero might just take a minute or two before you can start to create your characters. If there are different expectations about the game however you'll be glad to discuss them at the beginning of your journey together.

Overall, Session Zero is a valuable part of the game preparation process and can help to set the stage for a successful and enjoyable tabletop role-playing game. It's also a great idea to check in every once in a while if the decisions you made in the beginning are still fun for everybody.
