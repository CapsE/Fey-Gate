Characters and monsters have resolve that will be reduced before taking damage. It's easy to remove resolve. It's easy to regain resolve. Characters and monsters have 3 actions each turn they can choose from the following list:
1. Attack (Once/Turn): Roll to hit. If you meet or beat the defenders DR you either remove resolve or deal damage when none is left.
3. Move: Move up to your movement speed
4. Cast a Spell (Once/Turn): This can take more than one action. You don't need to cast the whole spell in one round but if you start casting a new one the progress is lost.
5. Intimidate (Once/Turn): Roll an intimidation check. If you beat 10 + the targets Wisdom modifier remove 1d4 resolve. Range: 15ft. If you are within 5ft. of an enemy and try to intimidate a target that is not within 5ft. of you, you do so with disadvantage.
6. Decieve (Once/Turn): Roll a deception check. If you beat 10 + the targets Intelligence modifier remove 1d4 resolve. Range: 15ft. If you are within 5ft. of an enemy and try to deceive a target that is not within 5ft. of you, you do so with disadvantage.
7. Grapple: Roll a strength check. If you beat 10 + the targets Strength or Dexterity modifier (pick the higher one) the target can't move until it breaks free. The target also looses 1d4 resolve. You can only grapple/restrain one target at a time. Range: Melee
8. Restrain: Target a creature that's already grappled and roll a strength check. If you beat 10 + the targets Strength or Dexterity modifier (pick the higher one) the target is restrained (It can't move or use it's "Attack" action, attacks against it ignore resolve). While you are restraining a target you can't use the "Attack" and "Cast a Spell"- Action and you can only move half your movement (dragging your target with you). You can only grapple/restrain one target at a time.
9. Break free: When you are grappled or restrained roll a strength or dexterity check. If you beat 10 + the Strength modifier of the grappler you are free.
10. Restore: Roll a d20 if you roll a 10+ you regain 1d4 resolve. If you roll a 20+ you regain 2d4 resolve
11. Make it count: This action allows you to add another d20 to your next d20 check and take the higher result.

### Resolve
Resolve is meant as a measure of combat awareness and the ability to stand ones ground against the enemy. It should provide a narrative different from D&D in which characters are not constantly wounded an continue fighting at full strength but rather try to outsmart or overpower each other, often working as a team until one of the opponents leaves a gap in their defense and takes a hit that actually does damage.
This new narrative allows for a wide range of new ways to harm and support combatants. It's hard to explain how the battle cry of a trusted leader can close the wounds of the soldiers around them but with resolve it's easy to convert this situation that makes narrative sense into the rules of combat.

### Health Points
Once the resolve is gone things get serious quickly. A hit or two are usually enough to take you from a confident fighting position into an early grave. Resolve is something meant to be gambled with. You should constantly ask yourself "Do I keep attacking them in the hopes to end this sooner than later or should I switch to a defensive approach?" but when it comes down to Health Points you either retreat and gather yourself or you're likely to die a heroes death.

When a character falls below zero HP they fall unconcious at the start of their next turn they begin bleeding out. Their allies now have one round to heal them back to at least 1HP or they'll die. Even when a character is healed they are only stablelized but still unconcious. Until they regain at least half their HP (rounded down) either through healing or resting they are considered wounded and their maximum resolve is halfed (rounded down).

### Defense Rating
Your Defense Rating (DR) is a combination of your ability to dodge and avoid attacks and the kind of armor you're wearing. Without armor it's calculated as 10 + your Dexterity. Lighter armor will ususally just increase this value while heavy armor might reduce the amount of DR you gain from dexterity but give you a higher flat bonus instead. Some amor might be __unflexible__ wearing this kind of amor gives you disadvantage on all skill checks involving Dexterity and Athletics.

### Critical Hits

When rolling a d20, the number rolled before adding any modifiers is considered the natural number. Rolling a 20 is a natural 20, a 1 is a natural 1, and so on. In combat, rolling a natural 20 is considered to be a critical hit. On the other hand, rolling a natural 1 is a guaranteed failure. There are effects that increase your chances of a critical hit and certain spells and maneuvers have a different chance of a critical hit. For example, if you get a critical hit chance of +3, you will land a critical hit on a natural 20 (as usual), 19, 18, and 17. Keep in mind that it is always the natural number before modifiers that counts.

A critical hit always hits, regardless of the enemy's Defense Rating. It also doubles the dice rolled when rolling for damage. If a target still has resolve, you can opt to do normal damage to its HP instead of double damage to its resolve.
